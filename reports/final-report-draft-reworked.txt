3. Introduction and Problem Statement
3.1. Introduction
Aircraft continue to be utilized in contested environments in which damage can often be sustained to an aircraft, causing critical mission failure, risking high-value assets, and causing undue loss of life. In the short term, there is a need for human pilots to assess their damage and capable maneuverability, in the long term, there is a need to design data-informed SHM systems that can be integrated into future autonomous intelligent aircraft, enabling them to revolutionize deployment in contested domains.
Aircraft operating in these contested environments without proper ability to estimate their sustained damage and maximum viable maneuverability remain vulnerable to overestimation of modified capabilities resulting in critical failures and underestimation of modified capabilities resulting in overly-conservative trajectory planning in time-critical deployments.
3.2. Problem Statement
As aircraft continue to see deployment in contested environments, they are likely to receive damage, and pilots must perform complex evasive maneuvers to prevent continued damage and continue operation. Pilots must assess the damage to their aircraft accurately through Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) in order to effectively plan these maneuvers and prevent critical aircraft failure while utilizing their full flight capabilities, requiring a quantitative estimation of maneuverable capabilities using measurable data onboard the aircraft. 
3.3. Motivation
In-flight damage assessment and load prediction requires estimating many complex internal and external dynamic factors, which requires gathering datasets of many modified aircraft that are subjected to severe flight-altering damage, then experimentally determining their new maximum g-limit. These costs lead to a large issue of data scarcity, indicating utility in simulated data and physically informed methods that perform better on smaller datasets.

6.4. Engineered Features

Mean Failure Strain (mm/mm)

Total Strain Energy at Failure (J)
Total Strain Energy Slope wrt. Load (J/N)
6.4.1. Strain Gauge ordering
Similarly to how the maximum element of a set carries more information and predictive value than an arbitrary element, we propose that by ordering the strain gauges, it is possible to analytically extract more information just by ordering the set. Therefore, it should be possible to order the strain gauges by magnitude across a given trial, and use the ordered elements as engineered features and their R^2 can be compared. Furthermore, their distribution should follow a learnable shape, which can be fitted to, then used as a more robust predictor to mitigate the sensor noise from a single measurement. We intend to show that (1) the ordering of the sensors improves their predictive ability from an arbitrary location, and (2) that by fitting a curve to their distribution that the fitted trend for the strain distribution curve can be used as a better predictor than the order of the sensor alone.

9.1. Overview
	Across all approaches, the Adj. R^2, MAE, and RMSE were found, then all models were sorted by their ability to meet those metrics.

Figure 1: Accuracy by Method
	As this system is implemented, an overprediction could be catastrophic while an underprediction reflects unused flight loading. For each approach, the actual predictions made can be linearly plotted and show the types of errors each model demonstrates. This verifies that LSTM, in addition to showing a poor R^2, also greatly overpredicts on a few key samples that could result in catastrophic failure, while many leading models remain bounded in their overpredictions. 

Figure 1: Residual Scatter by Method
	These overpredictions can then be plotted and quantify the implementation safety of each method by plotting their absolute maximum prediction and the top 1% of prediction values. This verifies that as a factor of safety is added to these predicted values, that the FOS is accurately fitted to capture the potential overestimation of the model. 

Figure 1: Absolute Error CDF
	This absolute error can be plotted as a cumulative distribution function (CDF) that shows the distribution of overestimates and the cumulative likelihood, where a perfect model would be a vertical line across the left axis, and curves horizontal length represents the error in Gs wrt. prediction error percentile. 

Figure 1: Over-Prediction Safety Margins
All of the results were plotted on a pareto front to show their accuracy wrt. inference time to show which models show promise for hardware implementation. LSTM and Random Forest are both immediately shadowed out by every other method on accuracy and speed. The PINN does technically have a lower accuracy so it does not totally deprecate the FFNN, but with their very similar performance, their difference can be neglected. All listed models are deprecated by GPR. The true accuracy-speed tradeoff exists between Linear Regression, Polynomial Regression, and Gaussian Process Regression. 

Figure 1: Full Model Pareto Front
The redundant models can be removed and the true trade-off can be identified between the top three competitive models: Linear Regression, Polynomial Regression, and Gaussian Process Regression.
